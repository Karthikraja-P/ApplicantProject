Project Euler — Problem 1
Multiples of 3 or 5
Find the sum of all the multiples of 3 or 5 below 1000.
Sum = multiples of 3 + multiples of 5 − multiples of 15 (inclusion-exclusion).
Project Euler — Problem 2
Even Fibonacci Numbers
By considering Fibonacci terms whose values do not exceed four million, find the sum of the even-valued terms.
Every third Fibonacci number is even. Accumulate even terms while the value ≤ 4,000,000.
Project Euler — Problem 3
Largest Prime Factor
What is the largest prime factor of the number 600851475143?
Trial-divide from 2 upward; the remaining value after all small factors are divided out is the answer.
Project Euler — Problem 4
Largest Palindrome Product
A palindromic number reads the same both ways. The largest palindrome made from the product of two 2-digit numbers is 9009 = 91 × 99. Find the largest palindrome made from the product of two 3-digit numbers.
Brute-force: iterate all pairs (a,b) with 999≥a≥b≥100, check if a×b is a palindrome, keep the max.
Project Euler — Problem 5
Smallest Multiple
2520 is the smallest number that can be divided by each of the numbers from 1 to 10 without any remainder. What is the smallest positive number that is evenly divisible by all of the numbers from 1 to 20?
Compute LCM(1, 2, …, 20). Use: LCM(a,b) = a×b / GCD(a,b).
Project Euler — Problem 6
Sum Square Difference
Find the difference between the sum of the squares of the first one hundred natural numbers and the square of the sum.
Sum = n(n+1)/2; Sum of squares = n(n+1)(2n+1)/6. Compute (sum)² − (sum of squares) for n=100.
Project Euler — Problem 7
10001st Prime
By listing the first six prime numbers: 2, 3, 5, 7, 11, and 13, we can see that the 6th prime is 13. What is the 10 001st prime number?
Use a Sieve of Eratosthenes up to ~120,000, or iterate and test each candidate for primality.
Project Euler — Problem 8
Largest Product in a Series
The four adjacent digits in the 1000-digit number that have the greatest product are 9×9×8×9 = 5832. Find the thirteen adjacent digits in the 1000-digit number that have the greatest product. (The 1000-digit number can be found at projecteuler.net/problem=8)
Slide a window of 13 digits; skip any window containing 0. Track the maximum product.
Project Euler — Problem 9
Special Pythagorean Triplet
There exists exactly one Pythagorean triplet (a < b < c) for which a + b + c = 1000. Find the product abc.
Iterate a from 1..333; for each a, iterate b from a+1; set c=1000−a−b; check a²+b²=c².
Project Euler — Problem 10
Summation of Primes
The sum of the primes below 10 is 2 + 3 + 5 + 7 = 17. Find the sum of all the primes below two million.
Use a Sieve of Eratosthenes up to 2,000,000 and sum all surviving (prime) values.
Project Euler — Problem 14
Longest Collatz Sequence
The Collatz rule: if n is even → n/2; if n is odd → 3n+1. Starting from 13: 13→40→20→10→5→16→8→4→2→1 (10 steps). Which starting number under one million produces the longest Collatz chain?
Use memoisation: store chain lengths already computed to avoid recomputation.
Project Euler — Problem 15
Lattice Paths
Starting in the top left corner of a 2×2 grid, and only being able to move right or down, there are exactly 6 routes to the bottom right corner. How many such routes are there through a 20×20 grid?
The answer is the central binomial coefficient C(40,20). Use big-integer arithmetic or Python's math.comb.
Project Euler — Problem 16
Power Digit Sum
2¹⁵ = 32768 and the sum of its digits is 3+2+7+6+8 = 26. What is the sum of the digits of the number 2¹⁰⁰⁰?
Compute 2^1000 as a big integer (Python: 2**1000), then sum its decimal digits.
Project Euler — Problem 18
Maximum Path Sum I
By starting at the top of the triangle below and moving to adjacent numbers on the row below, find the maximum total from top to bottom: 3 7 4 2 4 6 8 5 9 3 In this example the maximum sum is 3+7+4+9 = 23. Find the maximum total from top to bottom of the 15-row triangle at projecteuler.net/problem=18.
Dynamic programming from the bottom row upward: each cell = its value + max of the two cells below.
Project Euler — Problem 20
Factorial Digit Sum
n! means n × (n−1) × … × 3 × 2 × 1. For example, 10! = 3628800 and the sum of its digits is 3+6+2+8+8+0+0 = 27. Find the sum of the digits in the number 100!
Compute 100! as a big integer (Python: math.factorial(100)), then sum its decimal digits.
Project Euler — Problem 21
Amicable Numbers
Let d(n) be defined as the sum of proper divisors of n (numbers less than n which divide evenly into n). If d(a) = b and d(b) = a, where a ≠ b, then a and b are amicable numbers. Evaluate the sum of all the amicable numbers under 10000.
For each n < 10000, compute d(n), then check if d(d(n)) == n and d(n) ≠ n.
Project Euler — Problem 25
1000-digit Fibonacci Number
The Fibonacci sequence has F₁=1, F₂=1 as its first two terms. F₁₂ = 144 is the first term with three digits. What is the index of the first term in the Fibonacci sequence to contain 1000 digits?
Generate Fibonacci numbers with big integers and stop when len(str(F)) >= 1000. Count the index.
Project Euler — Problem 28
Number Spiral Diagonals
Starting with the number 1 and moving to the right in a clockwise direction, a 5×5 spiral is formed: 43 44 45 46 47 48 49 42 21 22 23 24 25 26 41 20 7 8 9 10 27 40 19 6 1 2 11 28 39 18 5 4 3 12 29 38 17 16 15 14 13 30 37 36 35 34 33 32 31 The sum of the diagonals is 101. What is the sum of the numbers on the diagonals in a 1001×1001 spiral?
The four corners of ring k (side length 2k+1) are: (2k+1)²−6k, (2k+1)²−4k, (2k+1)²−2k, (2k+1)². Sum over k=1..500.
Project Euler — Problem 29
Distinct Powers
Consider all integer combinations of aᵇ for 2 ≤ a ≤ 5 and 2 ≤ b ≤ 5: 2²=4, 2³=8, 2⁴=16, 2⁵=32 … If they are then placed in numerical order, with any repeats removed, we get 15 distinct terms. How many distinct terms are in the sequence generated by aᵇ for 2 ≤ a ≤ 100 and 2 ≤ b ≤ 100?
Add every a^b to a set (which deduplicates), then return the set's size.
Project Euler — Problem 48
Self Powers
The series 1¹ + 2² + 3³ + … + 10¹⁰ = 10405071317. Find the last ten digits of the series 1¹ + 2² + 3³ + … + 1000¹⁰⁰⁰.
Compute each n^n mod 10^10 and accumulate mod 10^10 to keep only the last ten digits.
